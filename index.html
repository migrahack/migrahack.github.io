PHILLY,
THE DEFIANT CHILD

On April 16, 2014, Philadelphia's mayor passed one of the most progressive anti-detainer orders in the country. No carve-outs, no exceptions. Here's the story of how the community won and how the fight continues.

APRIL 16, 2014

On this day, Philadelphia's then mayor Michael Nutter signed the Executive Order 1-14, a policy to prohibit the city from holding an individual for US Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) or notifying ICE of their presence in police custody without a judicial warrant.

Let's start with some basics.

A detainer is a 48-hour hold of an individual in a local police district, jail or prison.

A judicial warrant is a signed by a judge. ICE currently has no procedures that allows this.

4,098 detainers were issued in Philadelphia between October 3, 2007 and April 16, 2014.

THE BUILD-UP

On December 11, 2013, local immigrant rights organization Juntos gathered in front of the ICE office in Philadelphia, blocking vehicles exits to prevent the transfer of detained immigrants. This essentially shut down ICE for the day.

CALL FOR ACTION

They proclaimed Ni Una MÃ¡s, or Not 1 More, and called for an end to ICE holds, deportations and the Secure Communities Program.


Secure Communities is a federal program that began in 2008 by George W. Bush and escalated under President Obama. It required local jails and prisons to collaborate with ICE by holding people for 48 hours for ICE to interview and/or detain for deportation. This essentially forced local municipalities to shoulder the responsibility of federal immigration law.

COMMUNITY DEMANDS

On February 20, 2014, a coalition of community rights organizations announced the Philadelphia Family Unity Network to end all ICE holds in the city. The organizations involved included Juntos, One Love Movement, New Sanctuary Movement of Philadelphia, Victim/Witess Services of South Philadelphia and Pennsylvania Immigration and Citizenship Coalition.

MAKING HISTORY

For the first time in Philadelphia, immigration was discussed and debated at the city council hearing on March 12, 2014. Community members marched from South Philadelphia to City Hall to testify and take part in the historic moment.

THE SHIFT

Nutter signed the executive order on April 16, 2014 to amend the city's policy regarding ICE detainer requests. In his speech before the signing, Nutter stated that "Residents ... will not need to fear that interacting with their government will result in a detainer for themselves or their loved ones."

WHAT THIS MEANS

Philadelphia would no longer hold anyone on an ICE detainer nor would the city give notice to ICE of anyone's release from custody without an ICE warrant signed by a judge, effective immediately.


Other municipalities across the country had passed orders trying to drive a wedge between the local police and ICE. But in their orders, there were exceptions and loopholes so that detainers could still be honored legally. Being the most progressive, the Philadelphia order closed the loopholes and ended police and ICE collaboration in the city.

Decline of Philadelphia detainer requests per year

The numbers of detainer requests in Philadelphia had already been decreasing since 2011. But the order ensured that the detainer requests that were still coming by ICE were rejected by the local enforcement.

Within the next six months, over 200 municipalities across the country adopted a similar, no-loophole policy toward the detainer requests. This created a domino effect to the dismantling of secure communities.

No more Secure Communities

On November 14, 2014, Homeland Security Secretary Jeh Johnson conceded in a memo that the Secure Communities program essentially failed.


From the memo: "The program has attracted a great deal of criticism, is widely misunderstood, and is embroiled in litigation; its very name has become a symbol for general hostility toward the enforcement of our immigration laws. Governors, mayors, and state and local law enforcement officials ... have increasingly refused to cooperate with the program, and many have issued executive orders or signed laws prohibiting such cooperation. A number of federal courts have rejected the authority of state and local law enforcement agencies to detain immigrants pursuant to federal detainers issued under the current Secure Communities program."

PEP brings new loopholes

Almost immediately, Johnson announced the opt-in federal Priorities Enforcement Program (PEP). PEP allows ICE to issue a request to be notificed if someone of interest is in the city's custody and/or when that person will be released. Essentially, this program provided an exception to the seemingly exception-less orders that cities passed in 2014.

Both the Obama administration and Johnson began to pressure cities that had passed orders against ICE and police collaboration, including Philadelphia, to cooperate with ICE again. Up to now, Philadelphia remains a Sanctuary City but not without a lot of defense and organizing by the same communities that had been fighting before.
